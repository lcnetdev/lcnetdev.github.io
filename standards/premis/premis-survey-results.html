PREMIS Survey Results

The PREMIS Editorial Committee conducted an Impact of PREMIS Survey in 2018 to better understand the impact of PREMIS in the preservation community and guide the Editorial Committee's work in 2019.

This survey was administered by the PREMIS Editorial Committee to better understand the current impact of PREMIS. Since the release of the original PREMIS Data Dictionary in 2005, there has not been a formal poll of users. The last official survey related to PREMIS was conducted in 2003, resulting in the report Implementing Preservation Repositories for Digital Materials: Current Practice and Emerging Trends in the Cultural Heritage Community , which greatly informed the original Data Dictionary. Thirteen years after the release of the standard, the Editorial Committee agreed that it was an important time to step back and find out what we can learn from those who work with PREMIS, as well as from those who do not.

The specific goals of this survey were to help the committee understand:

  • PREMIS's reach across professions, sectors, and geographies
  • The size and variety of collections that leverage PREMIS
  • How PREMIS is used in relation to other standards/schemas
  • Whether people find limitations with the PREMIS model, schema vocabularies, or documentation
  • How technologies have adopted PREMIS By learning more about the user community, we hope to be able to perform more targeted outreach, to gain input for future versions of PREMIS, and to improve the available resources.

The Impact of PREMIS Survey was sent to various lists in the preservation community, including:

  • PIG list
  • DigiPres (ALA)
  • Digital Preservation (JISC)
  • Digital Curation (Google Group)
  • Australia/NZ state libraries
  • OZ Preserves
  • Samvera (Google Group)
  • PASIG discuss
  • AMIA list
  • IASA list
  • Summary of Results

Survey respondents were primarily from national, university, state, and local libraries and archives--in that order. Based on survey responses, PREMIS implementers are using PREMIS with the following resources/formats: audiovisual, still images, documents, databases, and websites--in that order-and are preserving digital collections ranging in size from 5TB to 1PB. 90% use PREMIS for digital collections only, while 10% use PREMIS for both digital and physical collections. Implementers are using PREMIS in various ways--to create, extract, and/or collect preservation metadata. Currently version 2 is most used, version 3 is moderately used, and version 1 is least used; 70% of those who currently use version 2 plan to move to version 3. Half of our 93 respondents use all aspects of PREMIS (data model, semantic units, rules, vocabularies) equally and of those who serialize PREMIS, 48% use the schema, 24% use a relational database, and 18% use the ontology. Regarding entities in the model: objects and events are heavily used, agents and rights are moderately used, and environment is least used. Most implementers aren't using conformance measures and/or don't understand them. 60% build PERMIS into their toolset, while 40% use an out-of-the box tool.

Questions regarding the usefulness of supporting documentation for PREMIS revealed that implementers find the Library of Congress website and the following documents: Understanding PREMIS, Digital Preservation for Metadata Practitioners, and the Guidelines for PREMIS with METS to be the most useful resources. These were considered the least useful: PREMIS Implementation Fairs, Conformance Implementation, and the PIG listserv.0

The main reason given for not adopting PREMIS was that it wasn't in the respondent's toolset. Other secondary reasons listed were: it's too complicated, conformance requirements are too high, and respondent's preservation metadata solution predates PREMIS. Survey respondents found the main limitations of PREMIS to be the model and supporting resources.

The PREMIS Editorial Committee has drawn the following conclusions from our 2018 survey results: We need to find better ways to communicate with you, the implementors. This may include a change in how PREMIS Implementation Fairs are designed and conducted. We also want to develop a more robust communication forum to expand and/or replace the PIG list. We will also plan to find methods of explaining the model. In addition, we want to improve supporting resources to better serve the PREMIS community. The Editorial Committee is drafting a roadmap and timeline for these efforts. Our goal is to have the roadmap and timeline approved by midsummer 2019.

Thank you to all who contributed to the survey. Your feedback is extremely valuable to us as it helps us better serve you and benefit the digital preservation community.
