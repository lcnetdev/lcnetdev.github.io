ISO 639 Joint Advisory Committee

Defining a Language Code for Bosnian in ISO 639-2

(15 Feb. 2000)

UNITED STATES GOVERNMENT

Memorandum


LIBRARY OF CONGRESS

TO: Rebecca Guenther, Librarian, Network Development and MARC Standards Office
DATE: February 14, 2000

VIA: John Van Oudenaren, Chief, European Division

FROM: Predrag Pajic, Reference Librarian

SUBJECT: Bosnian language

This paper examines the question of whether a separate Bosnian language can be said to exist as the language (and hence, literature) of the Bosnian people (South Slavic Muslims), for the purpose of creating a subject entry and code for use in the catalogue of the Library of Congress.

Proceeding from the premise that "The language of a people cannot be the subject of negotiation," as the Croatian academician Radoslav Katicic wrote in his article About the Croatian Literary Language (Jezik 35/1987, 2,pp. 33-38), we should attempt to understand the historical background of those who live in Bosnia and the language they speak.

The Slavs settled in Bosnia at the beginning of the 7th century, and until 1463 lived sometimes semi-independently, and sometimes in independent feudal states. In the beginning the territory of Bosnia comprised a very small area around the upper reaches of the River Bosnia at the same time Croats and Serbs were forming their own states. This small territory of Bosnia was intermittently ruled by these Serbian or Croatian states up to the 12th century, at which time it became a semi-independent vassal of Hungary under the ban (warlord-governor), Boric (1154-63). Later, in the 13th and 14th centuries, Bosnia expanded under the periodic governorship of Croatian and Serbian landowners who annexed to it many parts of neighboring feudal estates while remaining most of the time vassals of the Kingdom of Hungary. Under its most prosperous ruler, the first king of Bosnia Tvrtko I (1359-1391) who was crowned in the Serbian monastery of Milesevo as the king of Serbs and Bosnians, Bosnia was established as a kingdom and a separate south Slavic country with its own Bosnian church. This church was founded on the basis of the Bogomil movement, a Protestant-like sect that had separated from the Orthodox faith as early as the 10th century.

In 1463 Bosnia was occupied by the Turkish Sultan Mehmed II (1432- 1481) and became for the next four centuries part of the Ottoman Empire. About the same time Duke (in Hungarian "herczeg") Stjepan Vukcevic Kosaca lost his feudal lands that adjoined Bosnia, thus adding this dukedom "Herzegovina" to it as part of the Turkish empire. During the Turkish occupation a considerable number of the Serbian aristocracy and their servants who had been members of the Bosnian church, converted to Islam, thus creating a new national segment which later became the embryo of the Muslim nation or Bosnjaci.

In the meantime the Croatian population which considered the Bosnian church heretical remained within the Catholic faith, having the support of adjacent Croatian Catholic principalities.

In the 15th century groups of Sephardic Jews emigrated from Spain to Bosnia and Herzegovina, adding their religion and ethnicity to the Catholics (Croats), Muslims (later recognized as a nation), and Orthodox (Serbs).

When the Ottoman Empire finally disintegrated in the 19th century Bosnia and Herzegovina became an autonomous state within the Austro-Hungarian monarchy in accord with the 1878 Treaty of San Stefano. With the fall of Austria-Hungary in 1918 Bosnia was integrated into the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats, and Slovenes, which later changed its name to Yugoslavia. With Yugoslavia's collapse in World War II and the division of its territory among Hitler's allies, Bosnia and Herzegovina found itself part of the newly created Independent State of Croatia. Four years later with the capitulation of Germany, Bosnia and Herzegovina was established as one of six federal states within a new Yugoslavia under Communist rule. After the collapse of communism and on the basis of a 1991 referendum, Bosnia and Herzegovina declared itself an independent state, which was subsequently recognized internatonally on April 6, 1992. This triggered a civil war which ended with the 1995 Dayton agreement and Bosnia and Herzegovina was established with two entities: Federal Bosnia (consisting of Muslim and Croatian territory) and Republika Srpska, the Serbian entity), both under the political and military protection of the UN.

The language spoken by the new settlers of Bosnia in the 7th century was a Slavic language similar to all other Slavic languages. According to Traian Stoianovich, professor emeritus of Rutgers University (NIN December 30, 1999 p. 65), it is not known to what degree the language of these settlers was influenced by the languages of the tribes they found there, but, in any case, with the coming of Christianity, Latin was introduced in Bosnia. Nevertheless, literacy in the Slavic vernacular spread with the missionary work of Saints Cyril and Methodius in the 10th century. And during Bosnia's "golden age" in the 1300's the vernacular was the language used in the courts, schools and in the Bosnian Church. In 1463 the Ottoman Turkish language became the official language of Bosnia. Slavs who had converted to Islam and, thus, held local leadership positions under the Sultan accepted the Ottoman Turkish language but also integrated it into their own Slavic tongue.

The language and literature of the peoples of Bosnia and Herzegovina subsequently developed in accordance with their ethnic roots. The famous linguist of the 19th century, Vuk Karadzic, often called the father of modern Serbo-Croatian, used the language of the people of Herzegovina as the basis of his unified Serbo- Croatian language, one of the official languages of former Yugoslavia before its collapse in 1991.

With the establishment in 1991 of the Republic of Bosnia and Herzegovina, the question of language became the subject of political, cultural, and linguistic studies. However, the term "Bosnian language" is not a product of the newly established state. The present-day Bosnian linguist, Alija Isakovic, quotes many writers who favorably used the term from the Middle Ages to the early 19th century, such as Evlija Celebi (1660, Turkish traveler and historian), the Bosnian Franciscans M. Divkovic (1563-1631) and Andrija Kacic-Miosic (1704-1760), and the writer and educator Dositej Obradovic (1739-1811). The term "Bosnian language" was rarely used, however, during the period up to 1918 when Bosnia was under the Austro-Hungarian Empire.

In the fall of 1999 the prominent Sarajevo weekly Dani, in cooperation with USIS, organized a round-table conference with a number of members of the Bosnian Academy of Science, university professors, cultural workers from all segments of Bosnian society and of all nationalities. The participants agreed that the question of the Bosnian language is as complicated as their general political situation. But it was agreed that, since Croats and Serbs call their languages by their national name, Slavic Muslims (Bosnjak) should be able to do likewise.

During the conference, Bosnian academician and philosopher, Muhamed Filipovic, supported the thesis that all peoples of B&H speak ONE language but their use of it may differ from one nationality to the other and such differences should be encouraged. Considerable time was given to a discussion of the first Bosnian official spelling book (Pravopis bosanskog jezika), by Senahid Halilovic. The book was reviewed and approved by the Center for Bosnian Studies within Preporod, the Cultural Society of Bosnians. The author, in an extensive introductory text (155 pages) to the Pravopis, lists spelling rules intended to single out those words that differ from the words used by Croats or Serbs. He uses the same rules of grammar prevailing for Croatian or Serbian, but in the dictionary section he adds more Turkish words and inserts the letter "h" into Croatian or Serbian words. For example, the English word "coffee" in Croatian is "kava," in Serbian, "kafa," and Bosnian is listed as "kahva". The intention appears to be to list as many words as possible, regardless of whether they are in popular usage or not. In the first two pages of the Pravopis, 75 of the 130 words listed are of Turkish origin, 30 are of Croatian or Serbian origin, and the remaining 25 are of foreign origin for which there are words in Serbian or Croatian.

For all practical purposes, I believe we should make an attempt to recognize the existence of the "Bosnian language," a term used in past centuries and now an expression of identification of the Bosnian nation. This may be rather difficult, since there is already a problem in differentiating Serbian and Croatian as spoken in Bosnia. However, as noted above, the presence of Turkish words, the insertion of the letter "h" in Serbian or Croatian words, and, of course, the name (Christian or Muslim) of the author, as well as place of publication (the federal part of B&H or Republika Srpska) should be indicators of whether the publication is in the Bosnian language.
